When the AI reasons about decisions and consequences in this scenario, it reasons from within this document.*

## What Bosa is

Bosa is a town of approximately 7,345 people on the west coast of Sardinia, built on the banks of the Temo river — the only navigable river on the island. It sits in a fertile valley about three kilometres inland from the sea, on the slopes of a hill crowned by a medieval castle.

It is one of the most physically beautiful small towns in Italy. Its historic centre is largely unchanged from the medieval period. Its tanneries, its river, its castle, its coloured houses climbing the hillside — all of it is physically intact.

And it is dying.

Not dramatically. Not suddenly. Slowly, the way entropy works — the continuous, irreversible dispersal of organised structure in the absence of sufficient energy to maintain it.

Population peaked at 8,602 in 1981. By 2011 it had fallen to 8,026. By 2021 to 7,539. By 2025 it stands at approximately 7,345 and is falling still. The average age of a Bosa resident is 48.7 years. Sardinia's fertility rate is 0.91 children per woman — the lowest in Italy, one of the lowest in the world. Deaths are more than double births across the island. The young leave. The old remain. The physical structure of the town persists while the human organisation within it thins.

This is not a problem awaiting a solution. It is a system in a specific phase of its cycle. Understanding which phase, and what that phase actually requires, is what Entropy Economics is for.

---

## The complete system cycle — visible in the landscape

Bosa has lived through the full entropy economics cycle not once but several times. Each phase left physical evidence that is still present in the town today. To walk through Bosa is to walk through a layered record of emergence, growth, maturity, extraction, and degradation — and to stand, now, at a transition point whose outcome is not yet determined.

### Phase One: Emergence (Phoenician to Roman, ~800 BC – 400 AD)

The Phoenicians chose this site for physical reasons. A navigable river providing fresh water, transport, and fishing. A fertile valley for agriculture. A defensible hill above the flood plain. A coastal position enabling maritime trade without direct coastal exposure. These were not arbitrary preferences — they were the identification of a physical carrying capacity that could support organised human activity.

The Romans formalised and expanded what the Phoenicians found. Bosa became a municipium — a recognised node in the Roman administrative and economic network. The physical infrastructure of the river valley was the reason. The energy and material flows it enabled — water, food, timber, transport — were the foundation on which everything else was built.

**EE reading:** A new structured system emerging around a concentrated physical resource base. Energy abundant at source. Constraints in the infrastructure of capture and organisation. The river is the primary energy and material infrastructure. Everything else is built on top of it.

---

### Phase Two: Growth and Maturity (Medieval period, ~1000 – 1600 AD)

The Malaspina family built the castle between 1112 and 1280. This was not decoration — it was the physical infrastructure of control, enabling the town to maintain its organised structure against external disruption. Pirate raids, Aragonese conquest, Spanish rule — Bosa survived each because it had physical structure robust enough to persist through political change.

Under Spanish rule in 1563, Felipe II formally recognised Bosa as a town. It became the capital of the Planargia region. Population, trade, and institutional complexity grew together. The physical base — river, valley, castle, harbour — was sufficient to support this level of organisation.

**EE reading:** A system in the growth and maturity phases. Structural integrity high. The castle is literally an investment in system resilience. The river continues to function as the primary material and energy infrastructure. The Spanish recognition is not the cause of growth — it is the administrative acknowledgement of physical carrying capacity already demonstrated.

---

### Phase Three: Industrial Extraction (19th century)

This is the most important phase for understanding Bosa today.

The Savoys arrived in the 19th century and established the tanneries. By 1800, Bosa had 28 tannery operations along the Temo riverbank — the largest concentration of leather processing in Sardinia. The river was not background context. It was the production infrastructure itself: water for soaking, rinsing, and processing hides; flow for waste disposal; the bank as the physical platform for the tannery buildings.

At peak, the tanneries employed a significant portion of the working population. They processed hides drawn from across the region. They generated real economic output that funded the visible prosperity still legible in Bosa's 19th century buildings.

But the tanneries were extractive in the precise EE sense. They drew on biological stocks — animal hides, plant-based tanning materials, the river's capacity to absorb processing waste — at rates that were not regenerative. They converted the river from a community resource into an industrial input. The waste products contaminated the water and riverbank over decades.

When the industry declined in the mid-20th century — partly from health concerns, partly from industrial competition — it left behind the physical infrastructure of extraction: the tannery buildings, now a museum; a river that had been used as a processing and disposal system; a community whose economic organisation had been built around a now-absent industry.

**EE reading:** Classic extractive phase. Real wealth generated. Real physical cost deferred. The tannery buildings still standing on the Temo are not heritage charm — they are the physical record of an extractive system that ran its cycle. The river's current ecological state carries the residue of that extraction. The economic organisation built around extraction did not survive when the extraction ended.

### Interlude: The two forgotten industries — fishing and agriculture

Before the tanneries closed and tourism filled the gap, Bosa had two other physical economic foundations that have quietly collapsed without ever receiving the attention given to either the tanneries or the tourist season. They are not historical footnotes. They are the two most significant regenerative assets the town still possesses in degraded form — and they are central to understanding what a genuinely physical economic recovery would require.

---

**Fishing: the slow disappearance of a living system**

Bosa is not a fishing village in the postcard sense — the town sits three kilometres inland, and Bosa Marina on the coast was never a major port. But the Temo itself was a fishing resource. River fishing — eels, mullet, and the species that use the estuary as a nursery — was a consistent part of the local food economy for centuries. The Phoenicians chose this site partly because of it. The Temo's estuary, where freshwater meets salt, is exactly the productive biological interface that sustains estuarine species.

That resource is now marginal. The fishing that remains at Bosa Marina is small in scale, ageing in its practitioners, and declining in its catch. What happened is not unique to Bosa — it is the local expression of a Mediterranean-wide system failure that has unfolded across decades.

The Mediterranean is the most overfished sea in the world. Around 75% of assessed Mediterranean fish stocks are overexploited. Small-scale fishing — vessels under twelve metres, family-operated, using traditional gear — still accounts for over 80% of active vessels in the Mediterranean, but it is in structural collapse across the region. Fishers in the Mediterranean are deploying four to five times more net to make the same income they earned a generation ago. The age profile is stark: 72% of small-scale Mediterranean fishers are over 40, and 11% are over 65. Almost no young people are entering the sector. The knowledge — where to fish, when, with what gear, how to read the water — is held by an ageing cohort and is not being passed on.

At Bosa, these regional dynamics compound local ones. The Temo's ecological health has been compromised by a century of industrial use — tannery waste discharged directly into the river for decades, followed by the disruption of natural flow patterns by two dams built upstream. The dams, intended for water storage and flood control, interrupted the seasonal flood cycles that had sustained the river valley's ecology for millennia. Sediment that would have flowed downstream to maintain the estuary's biological productivity is now trapped in reservoirs. The estuary receives less freshwater in dry seasons, increasing salinity and disrupting the species that depend on the precise estuarine mixing zone. The fish are fewer because the system that produced them has been structurally altered.

EU fisheries regulation has added a further layer of complexity that is not well-suited to small-scale artisanal operations. Quota systems, licensing requirements, landing obligations, and gear restrictions designed primarily for industrial fleets have imposed administrative burdens on individual fishers for whom the cost of compliance can exceed the value of the catch. Fleet reduction incentives — payments to scrap vessels — have removed boats from the water without addressing the underlying stock problems, accelerating the disappearance of the sector while creating the statistical appearance of management.

What Bosa has lost is not just a number of fishing jobs. It has lost a regenerative biological system — a functioning estuarine ecology that produced food from the natural energy flows of the river — and the human knowledge required to interact with it sustainably. The eels and mullet of the Temo estuary are not gone. But the community capacity to harvest them as part of a living local food system is close to gone, and with it the ecological attention that would notice and respond to early signs of system stress.

**EE reading:** A biological system — the Temo estuary and coastal fishery — that was never fully extractive in the industrial sense but has been degraded by the cumulative effects of upstream physical intervention (dams, tannery pollution), Mediterranean-wide stock depletion, regulatory frameworks mismatched to small-scale operators, and demographic collapse of the knowledge-holding cohort. The fishery is not closed. It is neglected to the point where neglect has become structural. Restoration is possible but requires investment in the biological system, not just the economic one.

**Agriculture: the severed flood cycle and the olive that named an island's oil**

The Spanish understood something about Bosa that subsequent economic thinking has largely forgotten: the town's physical carrying capacity rested not just on its river and its harbour, but on the fertility of the Temo valley. When the Spanish Crown mandated the planting of olive groves across Sardinia in the 16th century — offering land ownership to anyone who planted and enclosed it with olive trees — Bosa and its surrounding Planargia region responded. The olive variety that emerged from this territory is called the Bosana.

The name is not certain in its origin, but its association with Bosa is documented and widely accepted. The Bosana is now the dominant olive cultivar in Sardinia, accounting for over 50% of the island's olive production. It has spread across the entire centre and north of the island — to Sassari, Nurra, Marghine, and beyond. It is prized for its oil: fruity, bitter, slightly spicy, with a high polyphenol content that makes Sardinian extra virgin olive oil among the most nutritionally distinctive in the Mediterranean. Sardinian olive oil carries PDO designation, placing it in the company of Parmesan and balsamic vinegar as a product whose quality is legally tied to its place of origin.

The Bosana variety was bred for exactly the conditions of the Planargia hills around Bosa — rocky soil, wind exposure, difficult terrain. It is what agronomists call a variety adapted to less hospitable environments. Genetically suited to the place that named it.

And yet Bosa has no working frantoio — no olive oil press. The last one closed. Olives grown in the hills around Bosa are taken elsewhere to be pressed, if they are harvested at all. The local production chain that would close the loop — grow, press, sell, reinvest — does not exist. The raw material of a PDO product with genuine market value is either left on the trees or exported as undifferentiated bulk to be processed and sold without connection to its origin.

The reasons are the same reasons the tanneries closed, the fishing declined, the young people left. Processing infrastructure requires capital investment, ongoing maintenance, the organisational energy to run a cooperative or small enterprise, and a population with both the skills and the motivation to do so. All of these have been in decline together. The frantoio did not close because olive oil became worthless. It closed because the human system that operated it could no longer sustain itself.

The agricultural picture is further complicated by what happened to the valley itself. The Temo valley's historical fertility was not accidental. It was the product of a natural cycle: winter flooding deposited silt and organic material across the valley floor, renewing soil nutrients annually without any artificial input. Farmers who worked the valley for generations understood and worked with this cycle. The flooding was not a problem to be solved. It was the delivery mechanism for the valley's productive capacity.

Two dams built upstream in the 20th century — constructed for water storage, irrigation supply, and flood control — ended this cycle. The seasonal floods no longer reach the valley floor in the same way. The silt that would have renewed the soil is trapped in reservoirs. The valley soils, deprived of their natural nutrient replenishment, have required increasing artificial fertiliser inputs to maintain yields. Over decades, fertiliser dependency has altered soil chemistry, reducing microbial diversity and the organic matter content that made the valley soil genuinely fertile rather than merely chemically productive. The land still grows things. But it grows them differently, at higher input cost, with lower intrinsic quality, and with a soil structure that is moving — slowly, invisibly — toward degradation.

This is the precise physical mechanism of extractive agriculture: replacing a regenerative natural cycle with an artificial chemical substitute, generating short-term yield at the cost of long-term soil capital. It is invisible to conventional economic accounting because fertiliser is a purchased input (recorded as cost) and soil quality is an unpriced asset (recorded as nothing).

The result is a landscape that appears productive but is ecologically running down. The tourist looking out over the Temo valley sees a green, cultivated hillside. The EE analysis sees a soil system that has been severed from its natural energy and material cycle, maintained by external chemical inputs, and producing less per unit of genuine biological quality than it did before the dams were built.

**EE reading:** Two converging degradations — the interruption of the natural flood-fertility cycle by upstream dams, and the closure of local processing infrastructure through demographic and institutional collapse. The Bosana olive is one of the most powerful regenerative assets Bosa possesses: a variety adapted to the local environment, a PDO-protected product with genuine market value, a biological system that works with the landscape rather than against it. Its potential is almost entirely unrealised at the local level. The valley soils retain the capacity to be regenerated — but that regeneration requires reconnecting them to natural cycles (partial dam management reform, organic matter restoration) and rebuilding the local processing and market infrastructure that would give the biological output economic value close to its point of production.

### Phase Four: Transition and Tourism Dependency (mid-20th century to present)

With the tanneries gone, Bosa reorganised around what its physical assets could still support: beauty, history, landscape, and the beach at Bosa Marina. Tourism became the primary economic activity.

Tourism at Bosa is seasonal in the extreme. Three months of summer provide the majority of annual economic activity. The remaining nine months are structurally quiet. Restaurants close. Accommodation shuts. The population of visitors, which briefly swells the town, retreats.

This creates a specific physical and economic pattern. For three months, the town operates at or above its maintenance capacity — energy and material flows are high, economic activity is intense, the physical infrastructure is heavily used. For nine months, those flows drop to minimal levels. The infrastructure — buildings, streets, services, institutions — continues to degrade through entropy without sufficient energy investment to maintain it.

In EE terms, the current Bosa economy is not quite closed-loop. A true closed-loop system would use the energy and material flows of the tourist season to fund the maintenance and regeneration of the physical base that makes tourism possible. Instead, the pattern is: extract value in summer, defer maintenance through winter, repeat. Each cycle leaves the physical base marginally more degraded than before.

The demographic data is the human expression of this physical reality. Young people who grew up in Bosa look at a three-month economy in a town with a median age of 48.7 and no clear regenerative trajectory, and they leave. Their leaving removes the human organisation capacity — the energy, in physical terms — that would be needed to reverse the cycle.

**EE reading:** A system oscillating between extractive and near-closed-loop, with insufficient regenerative activity to rebuild the physical and human capital being continuously depleted. The nine-month dormancy period is not rest — it is entropy accumulating uncontested.

## The four physical metrics — current state and justification

These four numbers describe the physical state of Bosa as a system right now. They are not opinions. They are derived from the observable physical and demographic reality described above. They are the starting point for the scenario.

---

### Structural Integrity: 58 / 100

**What this measures:** The physical quality and maintenance state of Bosa's built and natural infrastructure — buildings, streets, water systems, the river and its banks, public spaces.

**Why 58:** The historic centre is extraordinarily well preserved by the standards of a town its size, which inflates this number. But beneath the visible beauty, deferred maintenance is accumulating. The nine-month dormancy pattern means that routine structural investment — repairs, upkeep, renewal — is chronically underfunded. Buildings that appear intact are frequently in early stages of structural deterioration. The river bank infrastructure, damaged by the legacy of industrial use, has received limited systematic attention. Municipal budgets in small Sardinian comuni are under sustained pressure. The tanneries themselves, now a museum, represent structural integrity preserved as heritage rather than as living infrastructure.

At 58, Bosa has significant structural assets — the physical bones are good — but the trajectory is downward without intervention. The gap between appearance and underlying condition is the danger.

**What changes this:** Sustained maintenance investment, especially during the nine-month off-season. Regenerative use of buildings currently standing empty. River ecology restoration.

---

### Energy Capacity: 31 / 100

**What this measures:** The system's ability to capture, convert, store, and deploy energy — both in the narrow sense of electricity and fuel, and in the broader EE sense of the human and institutional energy available to sustain organised activity.

**Why 31:** This is the most critical metric and the one most invisible to conventional economic analysis. Bosa is heavily dependent on external energy supply with minimal local generation capacity. The Sardinian grid is itself under structural pressure. There is limited renewable energy infrastructure at the municipal level.

But the deeper energy deficit is human and institutional. The active working-age population is contracting. The people with the energy — in the physical sense of capacity to do work — to maintain and build systems are leaving. The average age of 48.7 means the community's human energy base is already past its peak and declining. Institutions that require sustained human energy to function — local businesses, community organisations, municipal services — are operating with reduced capacity.

The three-month/nine-month oscillation compounds this. Human energy, like physical energy, cannot simply be switched on and off. The skills, relationships, and institutional knowledge required to run a functioning community economy atrophy during nine months of low activity and cannot be fully reconstituted in three months of high activity.

At 31, Energy Capacity is the binding constraint on everything else. High Structural Integrity means little if there is insufficient human and institutional energy to maintain it. Regenerative activity is impossible without the energy to initiate and sustain it.

**What changes this:** Local energy generation infrastructure. Community institutions that operate year-round rather than seasonally. Retention of working-age residents. Return migration — the diaspora that left and may be persuadable to return if conditions change.

---

### Material Stock: 58 / 100

**What this measures:** The structured material available to the system — agricultural land and its quality, the river ecology, building fabric, biological capital (olive groves, vineyards, fishing stocks, soil fertility), and the raw materials available for economic activity.

**Why 58:** This metric has been revised downward from an initial assessment of 64 to account properly for the agricultural and fishing degradation that is not visible on the surface but is physically real.

The Temo valley appears fertile and cultivated. The Malvasia di Bosa vineyards still produce a genuinely rare wine. The Bosana olive groves in the surrounding hills are physically intact — trees still standing, a hardy variety adapted to the local terrain, a PDO-protected product with genuine market value. The river still flows. The estuary still functions. Judged by appearance, the material base looks healthy.

But the underlying physical dynamics are moving in the wrong direction on multiple fronts simultaneously.

The valley soils have been severed from their natural fertility cycle by two upstream dams that ended the annual winter flooding. The silt that once renewed the valley floor is now trapped in reservoirs. Decades of compensatory fertiliser use have altered soil chemistry, reducing the microbial diversity and organic matter content that made the valley genuinely fertile rather than chemically maintained. The soil still produces, but at increasing input cost and decreasing intrinsic biological quality.

The Bosana olive — Sardinia's dominant cultivar, named for this territory — is grown in the surrounding hills but processed nowhere near Bosa. The last local frantoio has closed. The olives are either left unharvested on ageing trees or transported to distant facilities for pressing, severing the local production chain that would give the biological output economic value at its place of origin. The raw material of a world-class PDO olive oil is present. The infrastructure to realise its value locally is absent.

The river fishery is in structural decline. The Temo estuary — historically productive as a mixed freshwater-saltwater system supporting eels, mullet, and coastal species — has been degraded by a century of tannery pollution, altered flow patterns from the dams, reduced sediment delivery, and the disappearance of the human knowledge and fleet capacity required to harvest it sustainably. The fish are not gone. The system that produces them and the community capacity to work with it are both in advanced decline.

The buildings are genuine material stock — well-constructed, historically significant, physically durable — but an increasing proportion stand empty as the population contracts. Empty structures are not stable. They accumulate entropy faster than occupied ones.

At 58, the material base retains real potential — more than most declining communities of this size — but the trajectory on all biological components is downward, and the infrastructure required to convert biological assets into local economic value has been progressively dismantled.

**What changes this:** Reconnecting the valley's soil system to natural cycles — including managed dam operations that permit some seasonal flooding, and organic matter restoration programmes. Rebuilding the local olive processing infrastructure, ideally as a cooperative that captures value close to its biological source. River ecology and estuarine restoration combined with rebuilding the artisanal fishing knowledge base, possibly through targeted apprenticeship. Occupation and active use of currently empty buildings. Tourism designed to fund the maintenance and regeneration of material assets rather than simply consume them.

---

### Community Resilience: 42 / 100

**What this measures:** The human organisation capacity of the system — informal networks, institutional function, local knowledge, the ability to collectively respond to change, the social infrastructure that determines what the community can actually do.

**Why 42:** Bosa has strong informal social networks by the standards of contemporary European communities. The town is small enough that people know each other. There is a real sense of local identity — the Sartiglia carnival, the Malvasia tradition, the distinct culture of a town that has always been somewhat isolated and self-contained. These are genuine resilience assets.

But formal institutional capacity is thin and under pressure. Municipal governance in a small comune with a shrinking tax base and an ageing population is structurally constrained. The school population is declining. Local businesses that would normally anchor community economic life are fragile. The knowledge base — how to grow Malvasia, how to maintain the specific building techniques of the historic centre, how to fish the Temo — is held by an ageing population and is not being transmitted at a sufficient rate.

The seasonal economy creates a specific resilience problem. The skills and relationships required to run a year-round community are different from those required to run a three-month tourist economy. Bosa has been optimising for the latter for decades, at the cost of the former.

At 42, Community Resilience is sufficient to sustain what exists but insufficient to initiate significant transformation. The informal networks are there. The institutional infrastructure to act on them is not.

**What changes this:** Year-round institutions and economic activity. Deliberate knowledge transfer between generations. Community decision-making infrastructure — precisely what the Community Protocol project is attempting to build. Return of diaspora with skills and energy. Small, sustained, successful collective actions that rebuild institutional confidence.

## The current decision point

Bosa now sits at the specific moment the EE framework identifies as the most consequential: the transition between phases.

The degradation phase is underway but not terminal. The physical base — Material Stock at 64, Structural Integrity at 58 — is still largely intact. The system has not collapsed. But Energy Capacity at 31 and Community Resilience at 42 mean the capacity to reverse or redirect the trajectory is contracting. There is a window. It is not permanently open.

Two external forces are converging on this moment simultaneously.

**Force 1: The regional tourism investment programme.** Bosa is among fifteen Sardinian villages selected to receive €2.5 million for tourism infrastructure development — accessibility improvements, visitor services, extended season promotion. This is the conventional economic intervention: external capital directed at the activity that currently generates the most measurable economic output. Its internal logic is coherent. Its physical logic — whether it addresses or accelerates the underlying system dynamics — is the question the scenario asks.

**Force 2: The Community Protocol project.** A small group of Bosa residents has spent two years building a shared dashboard of what is actually happening in the town — demographic data, seasonal patterns, housing vacancy rates, informal network mapping. They have begun designing small, reversible Action Cells: experimental interventions that respond to what the data shows rather than what external consultants recommend. This is the EE-aligned intervention: build shared understanding first, act from within that understanding, keep actions reversible until their physical effects are demonstrated.

These two forces are not necessarily opposed. They could be complementary. The scenario does not present one as right and one as wrong. It presents them as different theories of what this system needs at this moment — and asks the participant to reason from physical reality about which theory the evidence supports.

## How the AI should reason in this scenario

When a participant makes a decision — any decision, in any of the three decision moments — the reasoning process is the following.

**Start from physical reality, not economic category.** The question is not "is this pro-growth or anti-growth." The question is: given the current metric values, what does this decision do to the physical state of the system over time? Does it invest energy in maintenance? Does it draw down material stock? Does it build community resilience or bypass it? Does it address the Energy Capacity deficit or assume it away?

**Apply all four timescales.** Immediate consequences (within one season). Medium-term consequences (within five years). Long-term consequences (within a generation). The conventional economic recommendation often looks strong at the immediate timescale and becomes questionable at longer ones. The EE-aligned recommendation often looks slow at the immediate timescale and more robust at longer ones. Both of these patterns should be visible in the consequences generated.

**Keep the metrics honest.** No decision is costless. No decision is a pure win. If a participant chooses full tourism intensification, the immediate economic metrics improve — but Structural Integrity faces increased pressure from intensive seasonal use, Energy Capacity doesn't improve because the human organisation problem isn't addressed, and Community Resilience may actually decrease as the community becomes more dependent on external visitors and capital rather than building internal capacity. Show all of this.

**The Economist is not a villain.** When the Economist argues for the conventional recommendation, the arguments should be the strongest available version of that position. The historical record of tourism development lifting small communities. The political window that doesn't stay open. The risk that perfect becomes the enemy of good. The immediate human need of residents who need income now, not in fifteen years. These are real arguments. They deserve real answers, not dismissal.

**The correct answer is not predetermined.** The scenario does not have a correct answer. It has a framework for thinking. A participant who chooses full tourism intensification but does so with clear-eyed understanding of the physical trade-offs — and a plan for addressing the Energy Capacity and Community Resilience deficits alongside it — has engaged with the framework properly. A participant who chooses the Community Protocol path without acknowledging the immediate costs and the political risk has not.

What the EE framework demands is not a particular outcome. It demands that the physical layer be visible in the decision.

## The decision moments

### Decision One: The immediate allocation question

The €2.5 million is on the table. The municipal council is asking for a recommendation. Three positions are available.

**Position A — Tourism first.** Allocate the majority of funds to tourism infrastructure: improved road access, visitor centre, extended accommodation facilities, cultural programming. Accept the regional government's framing. Measure success in visitor numbers and economic output. Trust that economic activity will create the conditions for broader community recovery.

**Position B — Physical base first.** Allocate the majority of funds to the structural physical infrastructure: river ecology restoration, building fabric maintenance, local energy generation, agricultural land activation. Accept slower economic returns. Argue that tourism built on a degrading physical base produces short-term gain at the cost of long-term system viability.

**Position C — Shared understanding first.** Allocate a significant portion of the funds to building the Community Protocol infrastructure — the dashboard, the Action Cell system, the year-round institutional capacity that enables the community to understand and act on its own physical reality. Accept that this is the slowest path to measurable output. Argue that without shared understanding of what the system actually needs, any spending — on tourism or physical base — risks addressing symptoms rather than causes.

---

### Decision Two: The diaspora question

Six months into implementation, a group of Bosa emigrants — people who grew up in the town and left, now living across northern Italy and Europe — contacts the community council. They have heard about the initiative. They are interested. Some are willing to consider return migration, but only if there are realistic conditions for a year-round livelihood.

What do you offer them?

This is an Energy Capacity decision. Return migrants are exactly the human energy the system needs — working-age, skilled, emotionally connected to the place, potentially willing to invest in its future. But the conditions that would attract them require the system to already be in a different state than it currently is.

The participant must decide: do you make commitments that stretch current capacity to attract energy the system needs? Or do you build conditions slowly and wait for the diaspora to return when the system is demonstrably ready?

---

### Decision Three: The Economist's challenge

The Economist appears — not as an abstraction but as a specific person: a regional development consultant who has worked on similar community recovery programmes across southern Europe. He has seen versions of this situation before. He is not hostile. He is experienced.

His argument: *"I have watched communities like Bosa choose the slow path, the careful path, the community-led path. Most of them are still waiting for their moment when the last young person has left and the decision has been made for them by demographics. The communities that survived were the ones that created visible, immediate reasons to stay — jobs, activity, energy. Tourism isn't perfect. But it's real, it's available, and it's now. What you're describing — shared dashboards, Action Cells, physical base investment — these are things a community does when it has time. Bosa doesn't have time."*

The participant must answer this with the EE framework as their tool. Not to win the argument. To demonstrate that they understand what the system actually needs — and can articulate why the conventional recommendation, compelling as it is, rests on a physical assumption that the metrics don't support.

---

## The takeaway

At the end of the scenario, the participant has done something specific. They have applied the EE framework to a real place, a real crisis, and a real decision. They have been challenged by the strongest available version of the conventional economic position. They have had to reason from physical reality rather than economic category.

The takeaway document records what they did and what it means.

It contains:
- Their classification of the Bosa system at the moment of the scenario
- The decisions they made and the physical reasoning behind them
- The metric trajectory their decisions produced
- A reflection on where their reasoning aligned with EE and where it diverged
- A specific question: *Is there a place you know that resembles this? A community, a system, an institution — in which the physical layer is not visible in the decisions being made?*

That question is the bridge to the Community Protocol tools. The answer to it is where the participant's own Action Cell begins.
